Somaliland Has Been a Democracy for 33 Years. The World Still Won't Recognize It.

In 1991, while Somalia collapsed into civil war, one region made a different choice.

Somaliland declared independence. Held elections. Built institutions. Maintained peace. Printed its own currency. Established a functioning police force, a military, and a civil service.

It did everything a state is supposed to do.

And the world looked away.

33 Years of Being Invisible

Today, Somaliland has:

  • A functioning multiparty democracy with peaceful transfers of power
  • Its own passport, currency (Somaliland Shilling), and central bank
  • A standing army and coastguard
  • Hargeisa — a growing capital with a real economy
  • The Port of Berbera — one of the most strategically located deep-water ports in East Africa
  • A bilateral agreement with Ethiopia giving landlocked Ethiopia sea access
  • Diplomatic offices in the UK, EU, UAE, Ethiopia, and beyond

And yet — not a single UN member state officially recognizes Somaliland as a country. Not one.

The silence is not neutral. It is a choice. And the people of Somaliland are paying for it.

Why Recognition Matters — The Real Cost

Some people ask: "If Somaliland is already functioning, why does recognition matter?" Here is why it matters — because the absence of recognition creates real damage every single day.

🛂

Citizens travel on a Somali passport — issued by a government they rejected 33 years ago

🏦

Businesses cannot access international banking or export treaty frameworks

📋

Foreign investors face legal uncertainty — no recognized state means no enforceable contracts

💸

Aid routed through Mogadishu may never reach Hargeisa

The cost of non-recognition is not abstract. It is paid daily — by real people — in lost opportunity, legal ambiguity, and invisible suffering.

The Double Standard the World Won't Admit

The African Union's principle of preserving colonial-era borders was meant to prevent endless fragmentation. It has become a shield for bad governance.

Nation How They Separated International Recognition
South Sudan Separated from Sudan Recognized immediately
Kosovo Declared independence from Serbia 117 countries said yes
Eritrea Broke from Ethiopia Recognized within months
Somaliland Peaceful separation from a collapsed state + 33 years of democracy Zero recognition

The AU's position is not legal principle. It is political convenience.

The Geopolitical Reality

Here is what the world is actually calculating:

  • Somaliland sits at the mouth of the Gulf of Aden — one of the world's busiest shipping lanes
  • The Bab el-Mandeb strait carries 10% of global trade
  • The Port of Berbera is underdeveloped but could be one of the most powerful logistics hubs in Africa
  • Ethiopia — Africa's second most populous country — already signed a sea-access deal through Berbera
  • China, Turkey, UAE, and the United States are all quietly engaging and investing
They want the strategic asset. They do not want the political cost. Somaliland is being used. Not recognized.

What Recognition Would Actually Unlock

If even five major countries recognized Somaliland:

  • International banks could legally operate there
  • Foreign direct investment would surge immediately
  • Somaliland citizens could travel on their own passport
  • International contracts would be legally enforceable
  • The Port of Berbera could access global shipping finance and expansion capital
  • Somaliland could join trade blocs, development programs, and diplomatic frameworks

The port alone — fully developed — could generate hundreds of millions in annual revenue and position Somaliland as the logistics hub of East Africa. This is not a dream. It is a calculation that only requires political will to activate.

A Message to the International Community

You say you care about African democracy. You fund elections. You send observers. You issue statements.

But when a nation runs elections peacefully for 33 years, builds real institutions, and asks to be treated as a state — you go silent.

Somaliland does not need your charity. It needs your honesty.

Either your commitment to democracy is real — and Somaliland deserves recognition — or it is selective, and you should stop pretending otherwise.

A Message to Somaliland's Leadership

Recognition will not be given. It will be earned and demanded. The path forward requires:

  • A professional, modern diplomatic corps — not political appointees
  • A funded international lobbying strategy
  • Direct engagement with US Congress, EU Parliament, and African Union member states
  • A clear, consistent legal and constitutional case
  • Economic partnerships that give other nations reasons to recognize, not just sympathize
  • A unified national position — not internal fractures that give outsiders excuses

The window for recognition is opening — slowly. The Ethiopia deal created global attention. The Berbera Port is creating economic interest. The question is whether Somaliland's leadership will move with the speed and professionalism this moment demands.

Somaliland has done everything right for 33 years. It deserves to be on the map.
