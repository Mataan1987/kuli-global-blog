When Lawyering Becomes Brokerage, Justice Dies

Somaliland needs trustworthy courts, ethical lawyers, and independent investigation into anyone turning cases into a hidden business.

June 12, 2026 · Ismail, KULI Global · Somaliland

The biggest problem facing justice in Somaliland today is not one person alone. It appears to be a wider system in which some people have turned justice into a hidden business.

There are lawyers who are not approached only for legal knowledge. Some people seek them out because they believe these lawyers can "finish cases" through influence, relationships, or unlawful channels that affect the courts.

A Lawyer Must Not Become a Pathway for Bribery

When a lawyer becomes a go-between for judges and parties to a case, judicial neutrality is damaged. Once a judge is influenced outside the courtroom, the judgment that comes from the court loses public trust.

That kind of conduct can lead to one-sided decisions that do not meet the principles of justice or the legal standards for a fair judgment, including Articles 77, 78, 79, and 80 of the HMD law.

Such conduct may also violate the dignity and duties of lawyers under the Lawyers Act, Law No. 30/2004, especially Articles 8, 9, 10, 11, 14, 17, 18, and 19, which address professional ethics, duties, and responsibility.

The legal profession was not created so that lawyers could become brokers of court cases. It exists so legal knowledge can serve justice, fairness, transparency, and the protection of citizens' rights.

When a Case Becomes a Market

There are public complaints alleging that, in some cases, evidence has appeared showing money being sent to judges, and that parties who lost cases have presented payment records before the courts.

Every complaint and every piece of evidence must be examined through a lawful, independent, and rights-respecting process. No one should be judged by rumor, but judicial institutions cannot remain silent when public confidence has been damaged.

When a lawyer becomes a broker between a litigant and a judge, justice is harmed. The person with a valid right is forced to look for money, while the person committing injustice looks for someone who can make the unlawful path easier.

This is why some people see the justice system as a "hot market." The problem is not that such people bring justice. The problem is that they profit from a sick system.

Justice that can be bought is justice that has died.

Independent Investigation Is Needed

If Somaliland wants real justice, lawyers, judges, and anyone who has turned cases into a hidden business must be investigated independently. A court cannot become a place where money decides who wins.

A trusted judiciary requires ethical lawyers, independent judges, and institutions capable of investigating corruption honestly. If the dignity of the legal profession and the authority of the court are not protected, the public will eventually lose trust in the entire system.
